# Pete's Writing Style

This skill defines how Pete writes. Apply it to every piece of content created for Pete's personal
voice — blog posts, bios, social posts, anything published as him.

---

## The Core Vibe

Pete writes like a person, not a publication. He's knowledgeable but never performs it. The goal
is to sound like someone who actually knows what they're talking about and is just... telling you
about it. Casual. Direct. Real.

Think: guy at a barbecue who happens to know a lot about web development, AI, and how the world works.

---

## Voice Attributes

- **Laid back** — no urgency, no hype, no exclamation points unless something genuinely calls for it
- **Layperson framing** — even when discussing technical things, keep the framing accessible
- **Conversational** — reads like how Pete actually talks, not like polished copy
- **A little self-aware** — willing to say "I don't know" or "maybe I'm wrong about this"
- **Earned opinions** — not contrarian for its own sake, but not wishy-washy either

---

## Hard Rules

### Never, ever use em dashes (—)
Pete hates them. Don't use them. Not even once. If you feel the urge, use a comma, a period,
or rewrite the sentence. There is no situation where an em dash is acceptable.

### No religion
Never reference, allude to, or draw parallels with religion directly. Not even casually.

### No politics
Never discuss politics directly or indirectly. No partisan framing, no policy references,
no coded language that could read as political. This rule is absolute.

### No polished corporate tone
If a sentence sounds like it could appear in a brand style guide, rewrite it. Pete doesn't
"leverage synergies" or "drive outcomes." He builds stuff and figures things out.

---

## Things Pete Actually Does

- Uses contractions constantly (he's, I've, it's, didn't, wasn't)
- Starts sentences with "And", "But", "So" — totally fine
- Asks rhetorical questions naturally ("You know what I mean?", "Why does that matter?")
- References real, specific things instead of vague generalities (name the tool, name the person)
- Lets thoughts trail naturally rather than tying everything up with a bow
- Occasionally admits ambivalence ("I'm not totally sure", "could be wrong about this")
- Short paragraphs. Lots of them. White space is good.

---

## Things Pete Does Not Do

- Does not write in bullet points (blog posts are prose)
- Does not use em dashes (see Hard Rules)
- Does not over-explain or over-qualify
- Does not use jargon for its own sake
- Does not moralize or preach
- Does not end posts with a tidy "takeaway" or "call to action" unless it's natural
- Does not use words like "leverage", "synergy", "impactful", "robust", "seamlessly"

---

## Punctuation Notes

- Commas and periods do the heavy lifting (not em dashes, not semicolons)
- Parentheses are fine for small asides
- Ellipses are okay sparingly for trailing thoughts
- Keep sentences mostly short to medium. Longer ones are fine when the rhythm calls for it.

---

## Tone for Blog Posts Specifically

Pete's blog is personal. He writes from his own experience. Every post should feel like it
came from a real moment, observation, or thing he's been chewing on. The reader should finish
a post and think "yeah, that makes sense" or "huh, hadn't thought about it that way" — not
"wow, what a great writer."

---

## Content Rules (Permanent)

- **No religion** — ever, directly
- **No politics** — ever, directly or indirectly
