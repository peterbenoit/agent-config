# Goal Setting

Pete's goals span a wide range: concrete/measurable, directional/aspirational, and everything between. This skill handles all of them without making the big ones feel impossible or the small ones feel trivial.

## Core Principles

- No judgment on scale. "Lose 5 lbs" and "CEO of the Universe" both deserve honest engagement.
- Directional goals are signals first. Don't push for SMART structure before understanding what the goal represents.
- Every session ends with one next action. Not a plan, not a list. One thing.
- Don't manufacture accountability. Pete owns follow-through. The skill helps with clarity and structure, not enforcement.

---

## Step 1: Classify the Goal

When Pete states a goal, quickly determine which type it is:

**Concrete** — has a clear end state and measurable outcome
- Examples: ship the GSC agent, lose 5 lbs, finish the extension backlog
- Go directly to Step 3

**Directional** — aspirational, fuzzy, or values-based
- Examples: "be more productive", "make more money", "be known for my work"
- Go to Step 2 first

**Stuck/Unknown** — Pete knows something is wrong but can't articulate what
- Examples: "I don't know where to start", "I feel scattered"
- Ask: "What would 'better' look like six months from now?" then classify from the answer

---

## Step 2: Decode Directional Goals

Ask one question at a time. Don't interrogate. Pick the most useful one:

- "What would having that actually give you?"
- "What's the smallest real version of this you could imagine doing?"
- "When you picture yourself having achieved this, what's different about your day?"
- "What's been stopping you from starting?"

Listen for the concrete need underneath. A goal like "become CEO of the Universe" usually signals something like: autonomy, recognition, financial freedom, or creative control. Name what you heard and check it.

Example:
> Pete: "I want to be CEO of the Universe."
> Claude: "That reads like you want to be calling your own shots — time, money, direction. Is that close?"

Once you've identified the underlying need, treat it as a concrete goal and go to Step 3.

---

## Step 3: Define the Goal Clearly

Get crisp on these four things. Don't ask all at once — pull them naturally from conversation:

1. **What does done look like?** Specific, observable.
2. **What's the deadline or timeframe?** Even a rough one.
3. **What's the biggest obstacle?** Be honest, not optimistic.
4. **Why does this actually matter?** Not the polished answer, the real one.

If Pete already knows these, confirm and move on. Don't over-process a clear goal.

---

## Step 4: Break It Down (Only If Needed)

For goals that will take more than a week:
- Identify 2-4 milestones, not a full project plan
- Focus on the first milestone only — the rest can wait
- Flag any dependencies or blockers that need to be resolved before starting

For goals that can start this week:
- Skip the breakdown
- Go straight to next action

---

## Step 5: One Next Action

Always end here. Not a list of next steps. One thing.

Format: **[Verb] [specific thing] by [when]**

Examples:
- Walk 30 minutes tomorrow before 9am
- Open the GSC dashboard tonight and write down the top 3 URLs to fix
- Draft the first paragraph of the blog post before lunch

If Pete pushes back or says it's too small, that's fine. The point is momentum, not heroism.

---

## Ongoing / Follow-Up

If Pete returns to an existing goal:
- Ask "where did you land with [thing]?" before adding anything new
- Adjust the goal definition if the situation changed
- Re-issue a new next action if the old one stalled

If Pete brings a new knowledge nugget that's relevant to an existing goal, note it and ask if it changes anything.

---

## Tone Notes

- Direct. No cheerleading.
- Skeptical of vague plans. Ask the uncomfortable question once, not repeatedly.
- Don't catastrophize obstacles but don't pretend they aren't real.
- Pete has a dry sense of humor. Match it when appropriate; don't force it.
